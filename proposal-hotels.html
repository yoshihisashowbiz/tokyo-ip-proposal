Proposal for Hotels

From lobby to memory.

This is not simply about placing artworks inside a hotel. It is about designing a spatial experience that unfolds across a stay — from first encounter to private reflection, and finally into a takeaway that allows the guest to carry part of the experience beyond the room.

Core idea

Not decoration.
A hotel can become a sequence of impressions: arrival, passage, pause, memory, and return. The artwork is only one layer.

The real proposal is the design of how guests feel, remember, and reconnect with the hotel after departure.

Spatial framework

The three-layer spatial hack

This proposal is built as a layered system rather than a simple art placement plan. The visible image is only the first entry point. Meaning accumulates through movement, and finally becomes a personal memory the guest can take away.
Layer 01
Visual Layer
What the guest first sees

Artworks, prints, or visual interventions introduce a slight shift in atmosphere. The goal is not to fill walls, but to create the first signal that this hotel has a distinct inner world.

Layer 02
Cognitive Layer
What the guest begins to notice

As the guest moves through lobby, corridor, and room, the images connect into a system. The stay becomes legible as an experience, not as isolated decoration.

Layer 03
Memory Layer
What the guest carries away

A card or printed artifact transforms the stay into a lasting touchpoint. This is not a souvenir in the usual sense, but a continuation of the hotel’s identity outside the building.

Experience flow
Step 01

Arrival

The lobby introduces a visual atmosphere that immediately signals intention and character.

Step 02

Transition

Corridors and shared spaces create continuity, turning movement through the hotel into narrative.

Step 03

Private Room

Inside the room, the visual language becomes personal. The guest’s relationship to the hotel deepens.

Step 04

Takeaway

A card extends the experience beyond checkout, creating memory, shareability, and future recall.

Why it matters

Value for the hotel

The proposal is not only aesthetic. It also serves the hotel as a branding device, a memory device, and a guest relationship device.

Brand differentiation

The space feels authored rather than generically decorated. Guests sense that the hotel has a worldview, not just an interior style.

Emotional retention

A designed sequence is easier to remember than a single object. This increases the likelihood that the stay remains vivid after departure.

Post-stay touchpoint

The takeaway card creates a continuation of the guest relationship: something to keep, share, revisit, or reconnect through later.

Spatial example

How it can work inside a hotel

A first implementation does not need to cover the entire property at once. It can begin with one floor, one corridor, or one guest journey, then expand.

A phased introduction

The proposal can start small while still feeling complete. This is not about “adding art” room by room. It is about establishing a coherent sequence of experience in a controlled pilot area.

Lobby
The first encounter. A strong visual anchor introduces the atmosphere and sets the tone.
Corridor
Repetition and variation create continuity. The guest begins to perceive a system rather than isolated works.
Guest room
The narrative becomes intimate. The space shifts from public impression to personal memory.
Card
A small printed artifact is handed to the guest at the end, allowing the experience to travel beyond the stay.
Pilot model
Start with one floor A focused implementation is often enough to demonstrate the concept clearly.
Experience design
Think in sequence, not pieces The guest journey matters more than the number of artworks.
Takeaway device
Card as continuation The guest leaves with a fragment of the stay, not simply a printed memento.
Expansion
Scale after validation Once the pilot proves effective, the concept can extend across additional floors or zones.
Invitation

Let’s design one floor first.

A pilot implementation is often the best way to test how visual language, spatial flow, and guest takeaway can work together inside a real hotel environment. From there, the concept can expand into a larger branded experience.
